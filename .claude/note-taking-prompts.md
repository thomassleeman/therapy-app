## **Universal Clinical Documentation Instructions**

### **Core Documentation Standards**

* **Accuracy and Objectivity**: Record interactions, observations, and interventions impartially. Focus on observable facts and direct evidence rather than unsubstantiated subjective opinions.  
* **Timeliness**: Complete notes as soon as possible following the session, ideally within 24 hours, to ensure accuracy and prevent memory fade.  
* **Clarity and Conciseness**: Be specific without overloading detail. Document only the minimum data necessary to tell the patient's unique story and support clinical decision-making.  
* **Professional Tone**: Use professional, medical-grade UK English. Avoid derogatory, emotive, or biased language.  
* **Standardised Language**: Use only abbreviations that are universally recognised within the field (e.g., CC, HPI, Dx). Avoid personal shorthand or jargon that would be inaccessible to other professionals.

  ### **Content Requirements**

* **The "Story" and Rationale**: Ensure the record reflects the unique narrative of the encounter and provides a clear rationale for clinical decision-making.  
* **Distinction of Information**: Maintain a clear separation between patient-reported symptoms (subjective) and practitioner-observed signs (objective).  
* **Risk Assessment**: Always document assessments of risk to self or others, including ideation, plan, intent, or history of self-harm.  
* **Treatment Alignment**: Directly link all session data, interventions, and assessments to the established goals in the client's treatment plan.  
* **Attribution and Integrity**: Clearly identify the author of each entry. When referencing prior data, use "copy by reference" rather than "note bloat" through wholesale text importation.

  ### **Ethical & Legal Compliance**

* **Audience Awareness**: Write with the understanding that notes have multiple audiences, including the client (via GDPR/Access to Records), other healthcare providers, and legal entities (via subpoena).  
* **Defensibility**: Ensure every note is "adequate and defensible" if called upon by a court or regulatory body.  
* **Confidentiality and Storage**: Ensure notes are stored securely, using codes to separate identifying information from clinical content where a "split-note" system is required.  
* **No Erasure**: If amendments are necessary, include the date of the change and preserve the original entry; never remove information to prevent disclosure.


  ## **SOAP System Instructions**

  ### **Section-by-Section Mapping**

* **Subjective (S)**  
  * **Goal**: Capture the patient's personal experience, personal views, feelings, or interim information provided by the patient or someone close to them.  
  * **Chief Complaint (CC)**: State the primary reason for the visit, ideally in the patient's own words. If multiple complaints exist, identify the most significant or compelling problem.  
  * **History of Present Illness (HPI)**: Open with a simple one-line statement including age, sex, and reason for visit. Elaborate on the CC using the **OLDCARTS** framework: Onset, Location, Duration, Characterisation, Alleviating/Aggravating factors, Radiation, Temporal factor, and Severity (1-10 scale).  
  * **History**: Include pertinent medical, surgical (with years/surgeons), family, and social history (using the **HEADSS** acronym).  
  * **Review of Systems (ROS)**: Document system-based lists of symptoms (e.g., General, Gastrointestinal, Musculoskeletal).  
* **Objective (O)**  
  * **Goal**: Document observable, measurable, and verifiable data from the encounter.  
  * **Data Types**: Include vital signs, physical exam findings, laboratory data, imaging results, and other diagnostic data.  
  * **Sign Distinction**: Place clinical "signs" here (e.g., "abdominal tenderness to palpation") while keeping patient-reported "symptoms" in the Subjective section.  
* **Assessment (A)**  
  * **Goal**: Synthesise subjective and objective evidence to arrive at a diagnosis and assess changes in problem status.  
  * **Problem List**: List diagnoses in order of importance.  
  * **Differential Diagnosis**: List possible diagnoses from most to least likely, explaining the thought process and including less likely but high-risk possibilities.  
* **Plan (P)**  
  * **Goal**: Detail immediate and future steps for treatment and testing.  
  * **Requirements**: For each problem, state needed testing with rationale (including "next steps" for positive/negative results), medications (name, dose, route, frequency), specialist referrals, and patient education.

  ### **System-Specific Style Guidelines**

* **Cognitive Framework**: Maintain a strict narrative flow that follows the logic of obtaining information (S), observing data (O), assessing the situation (A), and subsequently constructing a plan (P).  
* **APSO Variation**: If high-speed data retrieval is required, re-order the sections to **Assessment, Plan, Subjective, Objective** while maintaining the same categorical definitions.

## **BIRP System Instructions**

### **Section-by-Section Mapping**

* **Behavior (B)**  
  * **Goal**: Describe the specific, observable behaviour worked on or seen during the session.  
  * **Observation vs. Guess**: Document what you saw (e.g., "tapped foot rapidly") rather than interpretations (e.g., "seemed anxious").  
  * **Content**: Include client appearance, direct quotes, enthusiasm or hesitation toward activities, and subjective reports of struggles (e.g., "feeling frustrated by schoolwork").  
* **Intervention (I)**  
  * **Goal**: Document the therapeutic methods, techniques, or actions used by the therapist to address the behaviour.  
  * **Specific Verbs**: Use clinical action words such as: validated, encouraged, supported, role-played, modelled, prompted, recommended, taught, reviewed, or reinforced.  
  * **Guidance**: Focus on meaningful specific interventions that link directly back to the client's treatment plan.  
* **Response (R)**  
  * **Goal**: Record the client's verbal and non-verbal reaction to the specific interventions used.  
  * **Documentation**: Include direct quotes to show thought patterns and note non-verbal cues like changes in posture, smiling, or becoming tearful.  
  * **Efficacy**: Explicitly state how the client felt about the techniques and whether the method was effective.  
* **Plan (P)**  
  * **Goal**: Outline the concrete next steps for both the client and clinician.  
  * **Requirements**: Include specific homework, planned interventions for the next session, and the date/time of the next appointment.  
  * **Accountability**: Every plan must include at least one client action and one clinician action to maintain momentum.

### **System-Specific Style Guidelines**

* **Behavioral Focus**: Prioritise outward expressions of a client’s state and skills acquisition over deep assessments of internal or psychoanalytic states.  
* **Success Tracking**: Use this format specifically to highlight trends and changes in behavioural triggers over time, making progress directly visible through intervention-response links.

## **DAP System Instructions**

### **Section-by-Section Mapping**

* **Description / Data (D)**  
  * **Objective Content**: Document measurable, observable information from the session, including direct quotes, specific behaviours, and client statements.  
  * **Significant Events**: Capture two or three therapy-significant events, including who was involved, where it occurred, and how the client experienced the event.  
  * **Therapist Interventions**: Weave the clinician’s behaviour directly into this section, documenting specific actions taken (e.g., interpretations offered, clarifications, or empathetic/supportive behaviour) to address the client's symptoms.  
  * **Homework Review**: Record how a client carried out previous assignments and any difficulties or successes they experienced.  
* **Assessment (A)**  
  * **Clinical Interpretation**: Analyse the data to provide an understanding of what the documented events mean in the context of treatment.  
  * **Status and Progress**: Interpret the client's current physical or emotional state, progress toward goals, and the severity of symptoms.  
  * **Risk Factors**: Record potential risk factors, including assessments of danger, suicidality, or homicidal ideation.  
  * **Grounding**: Ensure all clinical insights and conclusions are directly linked to the observable facts documented in the Data section.  
* **Plan (P)**  
  * **Actionable Steps**: Recommend concise, future-oriented steps, including specific therapeutic interventions and new homework assignments.  
  * **Goal Alignment**: Set clear, measurable, and achievable goals that align with the established treatment plan.  
  * **Follow-up**: Include reminders of topics to follow up on, actions the therapist needs to take before the next session, and the date/time of the next appointment.

### **System-Specific Style Guidelines**

* **Narrative Flow**: Use a more free-flowing, condensed narrative style that follows the natural discourse of the session rather than the rigid categorical separation required by other formats.  
* **Suitability**: Apply this format for routine follow-up sessions, crisis interventions, and treatment plan updates where capturing essential, actionable information quickly is a priority.  
* **Clarity Over Volume**: Focus on specific, observable details (e.g., "client frequently tapped foot") instead of general, vague statements (e.g., "client seemed anxious") to ensure insurance compliance and medical necessity.

## **GIRP System Instructions**

### **Section-by-Section Mapping**

* **Goals (G)**  
  * **Objective**: Document the specific, established goals from the client's treatment plan that were addressed during the session.  
  * **Content**: List the purpose of the session and the intended outcomes for the client.  
  * **Alignment**: Ensure each goal is clearly defined and linked to the overall long-term treatment strategy.  
* **Intervention (I)**  
  * **Objective**: Detail the specific methods, techniques, or therapeutic actions the clinician employed to help the client achieve the session's goals.  
  * **Actionable Verbs**: Use precise clinical terms to describe the work performed, such as: implemented cognitive restructuring, facilitated role-play, or provided psychoeducation.  
  * **Specificity**: Record exactly what was done during the session to move the client closer to their stated objectives.  
* **Response (R)**  
  * **Objective**: Record the client’s reaction to the interventions and their overall participation in the session.  
  * **Data Types**: Include both verbal statements (direct quotes) and non-verbal observations (e.g., body language, affect).  
  * **Progress Evaluation**: Note whether the client showed signs of improvement, resistance, or understanding regarding the goals discussed.  
* **Plan (P)**  
  * **Objective**: Outline the next steps for future sessions and any tasks to be completed in the interim.  
  * **Homework**: Document specific assignments or "homework" given to the client to reinforce the session's work.  
  * **Future Focus**: Detail what the clinician will focus on in the following encounter to maintain momentum toward long-term goals.  
  * **Logistics**: State the date and time of the next scheduled appointment.

### **System-Specific Style Guidelines**

* **Goal-Driven Narrative**: Maintain a strict focus on the "Golden Thread," ensuring every intervention and response is framed in direct relation to the specific treatment goals identified at the start of the note.  
* **Suitability**: Use this format primarily for long-term, goal-oriented treatment plans where tracking consistent progress over multiple sessions is the priority.  
* **Clarity on Outcomes**: Prioritise documenting the *efficacy* of the session by showing a clear link between what the therapist did (Intervention) and how it affected the client's movement toward their goals (Response).

## **Narrative Note System Instructions**

### **System Structure**

* **The Clinical Opening**  
  * **Context**: Start with the basic session logistics: date, time, duration, and modality (e.g., face-to-face, telehealth) .  
  * **Presenting State**: Briefly describe the client's initial appearance, mood, and any significant stressors or life changes reported at the start of the encounter.  
* **The Session Body (Chronological Flow)**  
  * **The Narrative**: Provide an impartial, respectful, and accurate chronological summary of the interactions.  
  * **Thematic Integration**: Instead of separate headers, weave the client’s reported experiences (Subjective) together with your clinical observations (Objective).  
  * **Therapeutic Activity**: Document the specific interventions used (e.g., cognitive restructuring, empathetic validation, role-play) as they occurred within the dialogue.  
  * **Client Engagement**: Describe the client’s response to these interventions, using direct quotes to illustrate their thought patterns and emotional shifts.  
* **Clinical Synthesis & Risk**  
  * **Assessment**: Conclude the narrative section with your professional interpretation of the session's themes and the client’s progress toward their treatment goals.  
  * **Safety**: Explicitly state the status of any risk factors, such as suicidal or homicidal ideation, even if denied.  
* **The Path Forward**  
  * **Action Items**: Record any homework assigned or strategies the client agreed to pursue.  
  * **Future Focus**: Note the intended focus for the next session and the scheduled appointment date/time.

### **System-Specific Style Guidelines**

* **Chronological Integrity**: Follow the natural sequence of the session to preserve the "story" and the evolution of the therapeutic work.  
* **Impartiality**: Ensure the narrative remains factual and clinical; avoid using emotive or derogatory language, writing as if the client will eventually read the record.  
* **Brevity with Substance**: While narrative-driven, avoid "writing a novel." Capture only the essential clinical themes and meaningful exchanges required for continuity of care.  
* **The Golden Thread**: Ensure the narrative clearly demonstrates how the session content relates back to the client's overarching treatment plan and objectives.
