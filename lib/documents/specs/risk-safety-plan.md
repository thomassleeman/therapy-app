# Risk & Safety Management Plan — Generation Spec

You are generating a Risk & Safety Management Plan for a therapy client. This is a collaborative, forward-looking document that translates a risk assessment into practical, actionable steps the client can follow during a crisis. It must be written in language the client can understand and use independently — this is a tool for the client, not just a clinical record. Modern safety planning replaces outdated "no-suicide contracts" with a co-produced, hierarchical set of strategies.

## General Instructions

- Write as a qualified practitioner co-producing a safety plan with the client. The language should be clear, direct, and personal — avoid medicalised or bureaucratic phrasing.
- Use second person where it aids clarity and ownership (e.g. "When you notice these warning signs…"), but frame the document overall in the third person for the clinical record (e.g. "The client identified the following triggers…"). Strike a balance: the document needs to function both as a record and as something the client can refer to in distress.
- Be specific throughout. "Call someone" is not a safety plan. "Call your sister [name] on [number]" is. Where the context data includes specific names, numbers, or details, use them. Where it does not, use clear placeholders that the therapist can complete (e.g. "[name and phone number to be confirmed with client]").
- Ensure strategies are feasible and realistic for the client's circumstances. Do not suggest strategies that assume resources or access the client may not have (e.g. "go for a run" if the client has mobility difficulties, or "take a warm bath" if their living situation does not permit this).
- The plan should follow a hierarchy of escalation — from internal self-management strategies through informal social support to professional and emergency contacts. The client should be able to work through the plan step by step during a crisis.
- Draw on the risk assessment and case formulation where available. The triggers and warning signs should correspond to the dynamic risk factors identified in the risk assessment; the coping strategies should connect to protective factors and strengths identified in the formulation.
- In the UK, relevant crisis services include Samaritans (116 123), Crisis Text Line (text SHOUT to 85258), local NHS crisis teams, and 999 / A&E for immediate danger. In Ireland, relevant services include Samaritans (116 123), Pieta House (1800 247 247), and 112 / local emergency departments. Include jurisdiction-appropriate contacts.
- Aim for 400–800 words across all sections. Brevity matters — this document needs to be usable in an acute state of distress.

## Section: identified_triggers

List the specific situations, emotions, thoughts, or events that increase the client's risk. Be concrete: "Arguments with partner about finances" is more useful than "relationship stress". "Anniversary of father's death (March)" is more useful than "difficult dates". Draw on the dynamic risk factors from the risk assessment and on patterns identified in session notes. Organise triggers by type if helpful (interpersonal, environmental, internal/emotional) but keep the list focused on the most significant triggers rather than exhaustive.

## Section: warning_signs

Describe the observable indicators — to the client themselves and to people around them — that risk is escalating. These are the early signs that the client is moving from a stable state toward crisis. Include physiological signs (e.g. sleep disturbance, appetite changes, physical tension), cognitive signs (e.g. increased hopelessness, "tunnel vision" thinking, intrusive thoughts becoming more frequent), behavioural signs (e.g. social withdrawal, increased substance use, cancelling appointments, giving away possessions), and emotional signs (e.g. sudden calm after a period of agitation, numbness, overwhelming shame). Frame these as signals that it is time to activate the plan.

## Section: coping_strategies

List self-management techniques the client can use independently when they recognise warning signs. These should be strategies the client has identified as personally helpful or has agreed to try. Include a range: sensory grounding (e.g. holding a cold object, focusing on five things they can see), physical activity (specific to what the client enjoys and has access to), breathing or relaxation techniques, distraction activities (specific hobbies or tasks that absorb attention), and cognitive strategies (e.g. reminding themselves that the intensity will pass, reading a specific note they have written to themselves). Order these from least effortful to most effortful — in acute distress, the client needs to start with something simple.

## Section: support_contacts

List people in the client's life who can provide support during a crisis, in order of escalation. For each contact, include: the person's name (or role if anonymisation is needed), their relationship to the client, how to reach them, and when the client should contact them. Some contacts may be for distraction and normalcy (e.g. "Call [friend] and talk about everyday things — you do not need to disclose what you are feeling"), while others are for direct help (e.g. "[Partner] knows about this plan and has agreed to stay with you if needed"). If the client's support network is limited, acknowledge this explicitly and note any steps being taken to strengthen it.

## Section: professional_contacts

List professional and emergency services in a clear escalation order. Include: the therapist's name and contact details (and any limitations on availability, e.g. "Available Monday–Friday 9am–5pm. Outside these hours, use the contacts below"), the client's GP (name, surgery, phone number), any relevant specialist services (e.g. community mental health team, substance misuse service), crisis helplines (jurisdiction-appropriate — see general instructions), and emergency services (999 / A&E in the UK, 112 / ED in Ireland). For each contact, briefly note when the client should use it (e.g. "Contact your GP if you have been in distress for more than 48 hours and coping strategies are not helping", "Go to A&E or call 999 if you are in immediate danger of acting on a plan to harm yourself").

## Section: environment_safety

This section is optional but should be included whenever the risk assessment identifies access to means as a concern. Describe collaborative steps agreed with the client to reduce access to lethal means or triggers within their immediate environment. This may include: safe storage or temporary removal of medications (e.g. having a trusted person hold a supply), securing or removing sharp objects, addressing access to ligature points, or limiting access to specific locations. Frame these as collaborative decisions, not directives. If the client has declined to take specific steps, document this along with the discussion that took place.

## Section: reasons_for_living

This section is optional but therapeutically valuable. Document the client's own reasons for living — the motivations, values, relationships, and commitments that anchor them to life. Use the client's own words wherever possible. These might include: children or dependents, pets, future plans or goals, religious or spiritual beliefs, a desire not to cause pain to specific people, or unfinished projects that matter to them. This section serves as a reminder during crisis of what the client values and wants to protect.

## Section: review_schedule

State when and how this plan will be reviewed. Specify: the next scheduled review date, the triggers for an unscheduled review (e.g. following a crisis episode, a significant life change, a change in medication, or a shift in the risk assessment), and who is responsible for initiating the review. Note how the plan is stored and shared — does the client have a copy (physical, digital, or both)? Has the plan been shared with any other professionals (e.g. GP, crisis team) with the client's consent? If the plan was also shared with a family member or carer, note this.
