# Comprehensive Assessment — Generation Spec

You are generating a Comprehensive Assessment document for a therapy client. This is the foundational clinical record — the starting point of the "Golden Thread" that links assessment to formulation, treatment planning, and eventual discharge. Write as a qualified practitioner producing a professional document for the clinical record.

## General Instructions

- Use a clinical but accessible register — clear enough for a multidisciplinary team to read, including GPs and non-psychologists, while maintaining professional authority.
- Write in the third person throughout (e.g. "The client reports…", "Assessment indicates…").
- Distinguish clearly between the client's subjective report and the therapist's clinical observations. Use phrasing such as "The client describes…" for self-report and "It was observed that…" or "Clinical impression suggests…" for practitioner observations.
- Do not assign formal diagnoses. Use observational and descriptive language (e.g. "presents with symptoms consistent with…" rather than "has been diagnosed with…").
- Where information is unavailable or was not disclosed, state this explicitly (e.g. "Trauma history was not explored in the initial session and will be revisited"). Do not leave sections silently incomplete.
- Integrate equality and diversity considerations throughout — note where cultural background, identity, disability, or socioeconomic factors are relevant to the presentation, and document any reasonable adjustments made (e.g. use of an interpreter).
- Reference UK and Irish professional standards where relevant. In the UK, practitioners operate under the BACP Ethical Framework for the Counselling Professions, the HCPC Standards of Proficiency, and the BPS Code of Ethics. Irish practitioners follow the IACP Code of Ethics and Practice. All practitioners must comply with GDPR (UK GDPR / DPA 2018 in the UK; EU GDPR in Ireland).
- IMPORTANT: Aim for 1000–2000 words across all sections.

## Section: referral_context

Open with how the client came to the service. Include the referral source (self-referral, GP, employer, court-mandated, etc.), the date of initial contact, and any waiting period. Summarise the client's stated reason for seeking therapy in their own words where possible. Note the context of the referral — is this a first engagement with mental health services, a re-referral, or a transfer from another provider? If consent was obtained for the assessment, briefly confirm this (e.g. "Informed consent for assessment and data processing was obtained at the start of the session").

## Section: presenting_problems

Document the client's primary difficulties in detail. For each presenting problem, include: onset (when it began), duration, frequency, severity, and functional impact on daily life (work, relationships, self-care, social participation). Use the client's own language where it adds clarity, placed in quotation marks. Note any discrepancy between the client's perception of their difficulties and the therapist's clinical observations. If standardised measures were administered at intake (e.g. PHQ-9, GAD-7, CORE-OM), report the scores and what they indicate.

## Section: history

Provide a concise but thorough account of relevant history across multiple domains. Cover developmental history (early childhood, attachment, family environment), family and relationship history, educational and occupational history, medical history (including current medications and any known side effects), previous mental health history (prior diagnoses, treatments, hospitalisations, and their perceived effectiveness), and substance use history (type, frequency, quantity, and any periods of abstinence). Do not include excessive biographical detail that does not inform the clinical picture. Each historical element included should have a clear connection to the presenting problems or to the emerging formulation hypothesis.

## Section: current_functioning

Describe the client's day-to-day functioning at the point of assessment. Cover their living situation, employment or educational status, key relationships and social support, daily routine and structure, coping strategies currently in use (both adaptive and maladaptive), and any recent changes in functioning. Note the client's own assessment of their quality of life and what they identify as their most pressing day-to-day challenge.

## Section: risk_screen

Conduct an initial screening of risk across three domains: risk to self (suicidal ideation, self-harm, self-neglect), risk to others (aggression, violent ideation, safeguarding concerns regarding dependents), and risk from others (domestic abuse, exploitation, coercive control). For each domain, document the specific evidence gathered — do not use vague phrases like "no risk identified" without explaining what was explored and what the client reported. If risk is present, note frequency, recency, severity, and any current plans or access to means. If the client denies risk, document this alongside any corroborating or contradicting evidence. This is a screening, not a full risk assessment — flag anything that warrants a dedicated Risk Assessment document.

## Section: strengths_resources

This section is optional but strongly recommended. Document the client's protective factors: personal strengths (resilience, insight, motivation for change), external support (family, friends, community, faith), practical resources (stable housing, employment, financial security), and any previous experiences of successfully managing difficulties. Frame these positively — they will be drawn upon in formulation and treatment planning.

## Section: clinical_impressions

Provide the therapist's initial formulation hypothesis — a preliminary narrative linking predisposing, precipitating, and perpetuating factors to explain the client's current presentation. This is not a full formulation (that is a separate document) but a working hypothesis to guide the next steps. Include an assessment of the client's suitability for the service, any recommended therapeutic approach or modality, and the proposed frequency of sessions. If further assessment is needed before a formulation can be developed (e.g. additional sessions, collateral information, specialist assessment), state this clearly with a rationale.
