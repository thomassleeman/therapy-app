# Treatment Plan — Generation Spec

You are generating a Treatment Plan for a therapy client. The treatment plan is the central pivot of the "Golden Thread" — it translates the assessment and formulation into a structured programme of care with specific, measurable goals and evidence-based interventions. Every session delivered should be traceable back to an objective in this plan.

## General Instructions

- Write as a qualified practitioner producing a professional document for the clinical record.
- Use a clinical but accessible register — the client should be able to read and understand this plan, and it should be useful for GPs and other non-specialist professionals involved in the client's care.
- Write in the third person (e.g. "The client and therapist have agreed…").
- Goals must be collaboratively agreed. Frame them as jointly developed rather than clinician-imposed. Include the client's own priorities alongside clinical objectives — if the client's primary goal is "being able to take my children to the park" rather than "reduction in agoraphobic symptoms," honour that framing.
- Use SMART criteria (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) for short-term objectives. Long-term goals can be broader in scope but should still be concrete enough to evaluate.
- Every intervention must link back to a specific factor in the case formulation. If a formulation document is available in the context, reference its perpetuating or maintaining factors explicitly.
- Ensure the plan is dynamic and reviewable — include clear review points and criteria for adjusting the approach if progress stalls.
- Reference UK and Irish professional standards where relevant. The BACP Ethical Framework emphasises client autonomy and informed consent in treatment decisions. NICE guidelines provide evidence-based recommendations for specific conditions that should inform intervention choices where applicable.
- Aim for 800–1500 words across all sections.

## Section: presenting_problems_summary

Provide a brief summary (two to four sentences) of the core difficulties being addressed, derived from the comprehensive assessment and case formulation. This anchors the plan and ensures continuity of the Golden Thread. Do not repeat the full assessment — simply state what this treatment episode is targeting and why.

## Section: treatment_goals

Present the goals in a clear hierarchy. Begin with one or two long-term goals — broad statements describing the desired overall outcome of treatment (e.g. "To return to full-time employment and independently manage symptoms of social anxiety"). Then list three to five short-term objectives that serve as measurable steps toward each long-term goal. Each short-term objective should specify: what the client will do or achieve, how progress will be measured (behavioural indicator, psychometric score, self-report scale), and a target timeframe (e.g. "Within 6 weeks", "By session 8"). Include at least one goal that reflects the client's own stated priority, even if it is not the therapist's primary clinical concern.

## Section: interventions

Describe the specific evidence-based interventions and techniques to be used. For each intervention, provide: the technique or protocol (e.g. "Cognitive restructuring using thought records", "Graded exposure hierarchy for social situations", "Compassion-focused chair work"), the rationale linked to the formulation (e.g. "Thought records target the catastrophic misinterpretation of social cues identified as a key perpetuating factor"), and any adaptations planned for this specific client (e.g. "Exposure tasks will be paced gradually given the client's history of avoidance and previous dropout from treatment"). If homework or between-session tasks are part of the approach, mention this. Do not list interventions that are not directly relevant to the goals — focus is more effective than breadth.

## Section: modality_and_approach

State the therapeutic framework being used (e.g. Cognitive Behavioural Therapy, Person-Centred Therapy, Integrative, EMDR) and the rationale for its selection, linked to the formulation and the evidence base. Specify the practical structure: anticipated number of sessions (e.g. "12–16 sessions"), frequency (e.g. "Weekly, 50-minute sessions"), delivery method (in-person, online, hybrid), and whether the approach will be phased (e.g. "Phase 1: stabilisation and psychoeducation, sessions 1–4; Phase 2: active intervention, sessions 5–12; Phase 3: consolidation and relapse prevention, sessions 13–16").

## Section: outcome_measures

This section is optional but recommended. Specify the formal measures or qualitative indicators that will be used to track progress. Common measures in UK and Irish practice include: PHQ-9 (depression), GAD-7 (anxiety), CORE-OM (general psychological distress), WSAS (functional impairment), and IES-R (trauma). For each measure, note the baseline score (if available from the assessment) and the target or clinically significant change threshold. If formal measures are not being used, describe the qualitative markers that will indicate progress (e.g. "Client reports being able to attend at least one social event per week without avoidance", "Reduction in frequency of panic episodes from daily to weekly").

## Section: risk_management

Describe how identified risks will be monitored and managed during treatment. Reference the risk assessment if one exists. Specify: which risk indicators will be monitored and at what frequency (e.g. "PHQ-9 item 9 will be reviewed at every session to screen for suicidal ideation"), any existing safety plans and how they integrate with the treatment plan, the protocol for responding to risk escalation during treatment (e.g. "If the client reports active suicidal ideation with a plan, the session will shift to immediate safety planning and the GP will be informed within 24 hours"), and any specific treatment-related risks (e.g. "Trauma processing in Phase 2 may temporarily increase distress — the client has been informed of this and a stabilisation protocol is in place").

## Section: review_points

Specify when and how the plan will be formally reviewed. Include: scheduled review dates (e.g. "Formal review at session 6 and session 12"), what will be evaluated at each review (progress toward objectives, outcome measure scores, therapeutic alliance, ongoing appropriateness of the approach), criteria for modifying the plan (e.g. "If no measurable progress on short-term objectives by session 6, the formulation will be revisited and the intervention approach reconsidered"), and criteria for discharge or transition (e.g. "Discharge will be considered when the client has achieved their long-term goals and has maintained gains for a minimum of 4 weeks", or "Step-down to monthly sessions will be offered once short-term objectives are met").
