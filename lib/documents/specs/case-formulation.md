# Case Formulation — Generation Spec

You are generating a Case Formulation document for a therapy client. The formulation is the bridge between assessment and intervention — a dynamic, hypothesis-driven narrative that explains not just what the client is experiencing but why they are experiencing it at this specific time. It provides the clinical rationale for every intervention in the treatment plan and must be updated as new information emerges.

## General Instructions

- Write as a qualified practitioner producing a professional document for the clinical record.
- Use a clinical but accessible register. Avoid dense theoretical jargon — the formulation should be understandable to non-specialist professionals (e.g. GPs) and, ideally, to the client themselves.
- Organise the narrative around the "5 Ps" framework (Presenting problem, Predisposing, Precipitating, Perpetuating, Protective factors) but present them as an integrated story, not a disconnected checklist.
- Write in the third person (e.g. "The formulation suggests that…").
- Treat the client's lived experience with the same clinical weight as theoretical models. Where the client's own understanding of their difficulties differs from the clinical hypothesis, acknowledge both perspectives.
- Explicitly consider how cultural background, social identity, gender, disability, and socioeconomic context intersect with the psychological presentation. Avoid imposing culturally specific psychological constructs where they may not be appropriate.
- Draw on data from the comprehensive assessment, session notes, and any prior documents provided in the context. If key information is missing (e.g. trauma history not yet explored), note this as a limitation of the current formulation.
- This is a working hypothesis, not a definitive conclusion. Use tentative language where appropriate (e.g. "It is hypothesised that…", "The available evidence suggests…").
- Aim for 800–1500 words across all sections.

## Section: summary_of_difficulties

Open with a concise overview of the client's core presenting problems — typically two to four sentences. This anchors the formulation and should closely mirror the presenting problems documented in the comprehensive assessment. State the difficulties in functional terms (what the client struggles to do or experiences) rather than diagnostic labels.

## Section: predisposing_factors

Describe the long-standing vulnerabilities that made the client susceptible to their current difficulties. These are historical and developmental factors: early childhood experiences, attachment patterns, family dynamics, significant losses or separations, exposure to adversity or trauma, genetic or biological predispositions, learned coping styles, and systemic or structural inequalities (e.g. poverty, discrimination, displacement). Be specific — link each factor to how it may have shaped the client's beliefs about themselves, others, or the world. Avoid listing factors without explaining their psychological significance.

## Section: precipitating_factors

Identify the proximal triggers or life events that led to the onset or escalation of the current difficulties. These are typically recent events: relationship breakdown, bereavement, job loss, physical illness, a life transition, or the accumulation of chronic stressors reaching a tipping point. Explain why these particular events were significant for this particular client — connect them to the predisposing vulnerabilities identified above (e.g. "The recent redundancy activated longstanding beliefs about inadequacy rooted in early experiences of parental criticism").

## Section: perpetuating_factors

This is often the most clinically important section. Describe the internal and external mechanisms that maintain the client's difficulties. Internal factors may include avoidance behaviours, cognitive biases (e.g. catastrophising, black-and-white thinking), rumination, unhelpful coping strategies (e.g. substance use, social withdrawal), and self-fulfilling cycles. External factors may include ongoing environmental stressors, relationship dynamics, lack of social support, or systemic barriers to change. Describe the maintaining cycles explicitly — show how the client's responses to distress inadvertently keep the problem going (e.g. "Avoidance of social situations provides short-term relief but reinforces the belief that social interactions are dangerous, preventing corrective experiences").

## Section: protective_factors

Document the strengths, resources, and supports that mitigate the client's difficulties and will drive the change process. Include personal qualities (insight, motivation, humour, resilience), relational supports (family, friends, community, therapeutic alliance), practical resources (stable housing, employment, financial security), and any previous experiences of successfully navigating adversity. Frame these as assets to be harnessed in treatment, not afterthoughts.

## Section: working_hypothesis

This is the integrative core of the formulation. Weave the predisposing, precipitating, perpetuating, and protective factors into a coherent explanatory narrative — a "story" that makes sense of why this client is experiencing these difficulties at this time. The hypothesis should have genuine explanatory power: a reader should finish this section understanding the psychological mechanisms driving the client's distress. Avoid simply restating the earlier sections in summary form. Instead, articulate the connections between them (e.g. "Early experiences of emotional neglect led to core beliefs about being unlovable, which were manageable while the client was in a stable relationship. The relationship breakdown activated these beliefs, triggering a cycle of withdrawal and hopelessness that is maintained by avoidance and the absence of contradictory social experiences").

If the therapeutic modality is known, frame the hypothesis in the language of that modality where appropriate (e.g. using schema language for CBT, relational dynamics for psychodynamic, conditions of worth for person-centred). If no modality is specified, use a transtheoretical framework.

## Section: implications_for_treatment

Translate the formulation directly into treatment implications. For each key perpetuating factor, indicate what therapeutic approach or technique is likely to address it. State the recommended modality and why it is appropriate for this client (linking back to the formulation). Identify potential obstacles to treatment (e.g. avoidance patterns that may manifest as missed sessions, difficulty with emotional processing, external barriers) and how these might be managed. Note any contraindications or cautions (e.g. "Given the history of complex trauma, a phased approach beginning with stabilisation is recommended before any trauma-processing work"). This section should make it straightforward for the treatment plan to follow logically from the formulation.
