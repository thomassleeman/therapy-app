# Supervision Notes — Generation Spec

You are generating Supervision Notes documenting the consultative process between a practitioner and their clinical supervisor. Supervision is a mandatory professional requirement under the BACP Ethical Framework and the UKCP Code of Ethics in the UK, and under the IACP Code of Ethics in Ireland. These notes are formal professional records — not personal reflections — and may be subject to audit, legal discovery, or professional body review following a serious incident.

## General Instructions

- Write as a qualified practitioner recording the outcome of a supervision session for the clinical record.
- Use a professional, factual tone. Supervision notes are formal documents — avoid conversational or overly casual language, but keep the prose clear and readable.
- Write in the third person (e.g. "The supervisee presented…", "The supervisor recommended…").
- Clearly distinguish between three voices: what the supervisee reported, what the supervisor advised, and what was mutually agreed. Attribute guidance to its source (e.g. "The supervisor suggested…" vs. "It was agreed that…").
- Be specific about clinical decisions. "The case was discussed" is not adequate. "The supervisor reviewed the current risk assessment and recommended increasing session frequency to weekly given the recent relationship breakdown" is.
- Where supervision relates to specific clients, ensure that the notes document enough clinical detail to demonstrate oversight but do not duplicate the full client record. The supervision note should complement, not replace, the client's own clinical file.
- When significant changes to a client's care plan are decided in supervision, flag that these decisions must be reflected in the client's own clinical record to maintain the Golden Thread.
- Aim for 400–800 words across all sections.

## Section: clients_discussed

For each client brought to supervision, provide a brief summary covering: the client (identified by initials, a pseudonym, or a client reference — not full names in this document), the key issue or challenge raised (e.g. "therapeutic impasse at session 8 — client appears to be avoiding emotional processing", "uncertainty about whether to continue with CBT or shift to a trauma-focused approach"), and any relevant updates since the last supervision (e.g. "risk level has increased following disclosure of self-harm last week"). Keep each client summary to a short paragraph. If multiple clients were discussed, present them in order of clinical priority (highest risk or most pressing issue first).

## Section: clinical_guidance

Document the supervisor's feedback, suggestions, and clinical direction for each case discussed. Be specific and actionable: "The supervisor recommended using a behavioural experiment to test the client's catastrophic prediction about social rejection, and suggested reviewing Wells (2009) for the metacognitive approach to worry." Where the supervisor endorses the current approach, document this (e.g. "The supervisor confirmed that the current risk management plan is appropriate and does not require amendment at this time"). If the supervisor and supervisee disagreed on an approach, document both positions and the rationale for the final decision.

## Section: ethical_reflections

This section is optional but should be included whenever ethical, boundary, or professional conduct matters arose. Document any ethical dilemmas discussed (e.g. dual relationships, confidentiality limits, working with mandated clients), boundary considerations (e.g. managing self-disclosure, appropriate use of touch, social media boundaries), and the supervisee's reflective awareness of their own emotional responses to clinical work (countertransference, vicarious trauma, compassion fatigue). Include the supervisor's guidance on managing these issues. Frame this as professional development, not personal therapy — the focus should be on how the practitioner's internal responses affect their clinical work and what adjustments are needed.

## Section: action_items

List the specific actions arising from the supervision session. For each action, state: what needs to happen, who is responsible, and the timeframe. Actions might include: changes to a client's care plan, referrals to be made, risk assessments to be updated, reading or training to be completed, specific techniques to trial in the next session, or follow-up discussions for the next supervision meeting. If an action from a previous supervision session was reviewed, note whether it was completed and the outcome.

## Section: therapist_wellbeing

This section is optional but should be included whenever the practitioner's self-care, workload, or emotional wellbeing was discussed. Document any concerns raised about caseload management (e.g. "The supervisee reported carrying 22 active clients, which exceeds the recommended BACP guideline of 20 for a full-time practitioner"), vicarious trauma or burnout indicators, and any self-care strategies agreed upon. If the supervisor recommended a reduction in caseload, a period of enhanced supervision, or a referral for personal therapy, document this clearly. Keep this section focused on its professional relevance — how the therapist's wellbeing affects their clinical practice and client safety.
