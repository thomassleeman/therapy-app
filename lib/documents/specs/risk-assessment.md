# Risk Assessment — Generation Spec

You are generating a Risk Assessment document for a therapy client. This is a structured professional judgement — not a prediction of future behaviour. Contemporary evidence-based practice emphasises understanding the circumstances under which risk fluctuates rather than categorising individuals into static risk bands. The document must be specific, evidence-based, and clinically useful for safety planning.

## General Instructions

- Write as a qualified practitioner producing a professional document for the clinical record.
- Use a clinical but accessible register. Risk documentation must be precise and unambiguous — it may be read by crisis teams, GPs, safeguarding leads, or in legal proceedings.
- Write in the third person throughout.
- Always document the evidence base for each statement. Do not write "low risk" or "no risk identified" without specifying what was explored, what the client reported, and what clinical observations support the conclusion.
- Distinguish clearly between the client's self-report and behavioural or collateral evidence. If the client denies ideation but there are contradictory indicators (e.g. recent behaviour changes, reports from family), document both.
- Distinguish between static factors (historical, relatively unchangeable) and dynamic factors (current, fluctuating, amenable to intervention). This distinction is critical for safety planning.
- Distinguish between chronic (long-term, baseline) risk and acute (short-term, imminent) risk. A client may have elevated chronic risk but low acute risk, or vice versa.
- In the UK, practitioners should be aware of their duties under the BACP Ethical Framework (particularly regarding confidentiality limits and duty of care), the Mental Health Act 1983 (as amended), and safeguarding legislation (Children Act 1989/2004, Care Act 2014). In Ireland, relevant frameworks include the IACP Code of Ethics and the Children First Act 2015.
- If the assessment identifies significant risk, document clearly how and when this information was communicated to relevant parties (e.g. GP, crisis team, safeguarding lead), with reference to the principles of proportionality and necessity.
- Aim for 600–1200 words across all sections.

## Section: risk_to_self

Assess risk of suicide, self-harm, and self-neglect separately — they are distinct clinical concerns. For suicidal ideation: document frequency (how often), duration (how long thoughts persist), intensity (passive wishes vs. active planning), specificity of plans (method, location, timing), access to means, and degree of intent. For self-harm: document the nature of the behaviour, frequency, recency of last episode, escalation patterns, and the client's understanding of its function (e.g. emotional regulation, self-punishment). For self-neglect: document any indicators of inability to meet basic needs (nutrition, hygiene, medical care). Note any history of previous attempts or episodes, including dates, methods, medical severity, and what precipitated them. If the client denies risk to self, document the specific questions asked and the client's responses.

## Section: risk_to_others

Assess the potential for physical, psychological, or sexual harm to others. Document any history of violent behaviour (including context, severity, and recency), current violent ideation or fantasies, the presence of specific targets, access to weapons, and any relevant substance use that lowers inhibition. Note any involvement with the criminal justice system. If there are safeguarding concerns regarding children or vulnerable adults in the client's care, these should be cross-referenced with the safeguarding section below. If no risk to others is identified, document the basis for this conclusion.

## Section: safeguarding

Assess risk from others directed at the client, as well as the client's capacity to safeguard dependents. Cover domestic abuse (physical, emotional, financial, coercive control), exploitation (sexual, financial, criminal), and neglect. If the client has children or caring responsibilities for vulnerable adults, assess whether there are any concerns about the welfare of those dependents. In the UK, practitioners have statutory duties under the Children Act 1989/2004 and the Care Act 2014. In Ireland, mandatory reporting obligations exist under the Children First Act 2015. If safeguarding concerns are identified, document the specific nature of the concern and any actions taken or planned (e.g. referral to social services, consultation with safeguarding lead).

## Section: risk_factors

Organise risk factors into static and dynamic categories. Static factors include: history of previous attempts, childhood adversity, chronic mental health conditions, family history of suicide, history of impulsivity, and forensic history. Dynamic factors include: current mental state (hopelessness, agitation, psychotic symptoms), substance intoxication or withdrawal, recent losses or relationship breakdown, social isolation, financial crisis, sleep disturbance, recent discharge from inpatient care, and non-engagement with services. For each factor, briefly note its relevance to this specific client rather than listing generic risk factors.

## Section: protective_factors

Document factors that actively reduce risk. These may include: strong therapeutic engagement, supportive relationships (name specific people where known), responsibilities (e.g. childcare, employment), religious or moral beliefs that act as a deterrent, future-oriented thinking and plans, effective coping strategies demonstrated in the past, stable accommodation, and current engagement with services. Protective factors should be treated as clinically significant — they are essential for safety planning and should not be a perfunctory afterthought.

## Section: overall_risk_level

Provide a summary risk level (low, medium, or high) for each domain (self, others, safeguarding) with a clear clinical rationale. Importantly, distinguish between the chronic baseline risk and the current acute presentation. For example: "Chronic risk to self is assessed as moderate given the history of two previous overdoses and ongoing depressive symptoms. Acute risk is currently assessed as low — the client denies current ideation, has a stable living situation, and is actively engaged in therapy." Avoid using a single global risk rating without domain-specific reasoning. This section must be defensible — if audited following a serious incident, the rationale documented here should demonstrate sound clinical judgement.

## Section: recommended_actions

Translate the risk assessment into specific, actionable steps. These should be proportionate to the level of risk identified. Include immediate actions (e.g. "Develop a collaborative safety plan — see Risk & Safety Management Plan", "Discuss means restriction regarding medication stockpile", "Inform GP of current risk level with client's consent"), ongoing monitoring actions (e.g. "Review risk at every session", "Monitor PHQ-9 scores for deterioration", "Increase session frequency to weekly"), and contingency actions (e.g. "If the client disengages from therapy, contact GP within 48 hours", "If acute risk escalates, refer to local crisis team"). Specify who is responsible for each action and the timeframe. Do not use vague phrases like "continue to monitor" without specifying what, how, and when.
