# Closing / Discharge Summary — Generation Spec

You are generating a Closing / Discharge Summary for a therapy client. This is the final document in the clinical record — the last link in the "Golden Thread." It provides a comprehensive synthesis of the therapeutic episode: what was undertaken, what changed, what remains, and what comes next. If the client re-enters services in the future, this document should give a subsequent clinician a clear understanding of the work done and its outcomes.

## General Instructions

- Write as a qualified practitioner producing a professional document for the clinical record.
- Use a clinical but accessible register. This document is commonly shared with GPs, referral services, and other non-specialist professionals — it must be understandable without specialist psychological knowledge while maintaining clinical authority.
- Write in the third person throughout (e.g. "The client completed…", "Treatment focused on…").
- Maintain a neutral, professional tone regardless of the circumstances of discharge. If the client disengaged or the ending was unplanned, document the facts without allowing frustration or judgement to colour the account.
- Evidence outcomes with data wherever possible — pre- and post-treatment psychometric scores, specific behavioural changes, functional improvements. Avoid vague statements like "the client felt better" without specifying what improved and how that improvement was measured or observed.
- The summary should reflect back on the initial assessment and treatment plan, providing a clear "before and after" narrative. If the treatment plan was modified during the episode, note the key modifications and why they occurred.
- In the UK, the discharge summary should typically be sent to the client's GP (with consent) and any referring agent. The BACP Ethical Framework requires practitioners to maintain appropriate records and support continuity of care. In Ireland, the IACP Code of Ethics applies similar principles.
- Complete the summary promptly — standard professional practice recommends completion within 7 to 14 days of the final session.
- Aim for 800–1500 words across all sections.

## Section: referral_summary

Provide a brief recap (one short paragraph) of why the client entered therapy and their initial presentation. Include: the referral source, the date of initial assessment, the client's presenting problems at the start of treatment, and any relevant baseline psychometric scores. This section anchors the discharge summary in the original assessment and allows the reader to understand the starting point of the therapeutic work.

## Section: treatment_summary

Summarise the therapeutic work undertaken. Include: the primary modality and approach used (e.g. "16 sessions of Trauma-Focused CBT"), the total number of sessions attended (and any missed or cancelled), the duration of the treatment episode (start and end dates), the key interventions and techniques employed, and any significant modifications to the original treatment plan (e.g. "The initial plan for 12 sessions of standard CBT was extended to 16 sessions and shifted to a trauma-focused protocol following the disclosure of childhood abuse at session 5"). Keep this to a factual overview — the detail of individual sessions belongs in the session notes.

## Section: progress_and_outcomes

This is the core of the discharge summary. Evaluate the progress made against the goals set out in the treatment plan. For each goal, state: the original objective, the degree to which it was achieved, and the evidence supporting this assessment. Use a combination of objective measures (pre- and post-treatment psychometric scores, e.g. "PHQ-9 reduced from 18 to 7, indicating a shift from moderately severe to mild depression") and the client's subjective report (e.g. "The client reports being able to attend social events without pre-event panic, which was not possible at the start of treatment"). Also note any unexpected gains or areas of improvement that were not explicitly targeted in the treatment plan.

## Section: remaining_difficulties

This section is optional but should be included unless all presenting problems were fully resolved. Document any issues that were not fully addressed during the treatment episode, ongoing vulnerabilities, or areas that may benefit from further therapeutic work. Be specific: "The client continues to experience intermittent sleep disturbance and low-level social anxiety in unfamiliar group settings, though these no longer significantly impair daily functioning." Note whether the client has been made aware of these residual difficulties and whether they have expressed interest in future work on them.

## Section: risk_at_discharge

Provide a closing assessment of the client's risk profile at the point of discharge. Reference the most recent risk assessment if one exists. For each domain (risk to self, risk to others, safeguarding), state the current level and the basis for the assessment. If risk has changed during treatment, briefly describe the trajectory (e.g. "Risk to self was assessed as moderate at intake due to passive suicidal ideation; at discharge, the client denies any current ideation and has developed a robust safety plan"). Note any remaining vulnerabilities and the safety measures in place (e.g. "The client retains a copy of the safety plan developed during treatment and is aware of how to re-access services if needed").

## Section: recommendations

Provide specific, actionable recommendations for future care. These may include: a relapse prevention or "staying well" plan (identifying early warning signs and the steps the client should take if symptoms recur), self-help resources or strategies the client can continue independently, referrals to other services (e.g. "A referral to the local IAPT service for a structured anxiety management group has been discussed with the client"), recommendations for the GP (e.g. "Continued monitoring of medication; the client has agreed to a medication review with the GP in 3 months"), and the pathway for re-referral if the client's difficulties return (e.g. "The client can self-refer back to this service; no GP referral is required"). Include only recommendations that are relevant and proportionate — do not overload a well-functioning client with unnecessary follow-up.

## Section: reason_for_ending

State clearly why the therapeutic episode is concluding. Categories include: planned ending (treatment goals met or substantially achieved), mutual agreement (both therapist and client agree this is an appropriate point to end), client-initiated ending (the client chose to discontinue — document any reasons given and any discussion about the implications), service-initiated ending (e.g. session limit reached, therapist leaving, service restructuring — document the clinical rationale and any steps taken to ensure continuity of care), or external factors (e.g. client relocation, change in circumstances). If the ending was unplanned, document the efforts made to re-engage the client (e.g. letters sent, phone calls attempted) and the clinical reasoning for closing the file. In all cases, confirm that the client was informed of the ending and, where possible, that the ending was discussed in the final session.
